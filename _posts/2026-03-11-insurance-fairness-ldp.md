The FCA's EP25/2 consultation paper on pricing and protected characteristics, published in 2025, sets out a clear expectation: insurers should be able to demonstrate that their pricing does not produce systematically worse outcomes for people with protected characteristics. The ethnicity pricing gap is explicitly named. The consultation does not suggest this is optional.

Here is the problem every UK motor insurer faces. You cannot comply with that expectation if you cannot measure the gap. You cannot measure the gap if you do not have ethnicity data. You cannot hold ethnicity data — it is special category data under GDPR Article 9, and you have no lawful basis to process it for pricing purposes. Your legal team will not let you collect it. The ICO will not let you store it.

This is a genuine trap, not a theoretical one. The regulator expects you to prove your pricing is fair on a dimension you are prohibited from observing.

[`insurance-fairness-ldp`](https://github.com/burning-cost/insurance-fairness-ldp) is the way out. It implements the framework from Zhang, Liu & Shi (arXiv:2504.11775, April 2025): a policyholder or a trusted third party sends a *privatised* version of the sensitive attribute, generated by a local differential privacy mechanism. The insurer never sees the true value. The mathematics allows the insurer to recover the group-level statistics needed to audit and correct for discrimination — without that GDPR Article 9 exposure. 2,674 lines of source, 214 tests, MIT-licensed.

```bash

```

## The regulatory trap in more detail

The Test-Achats ruling (ECJ, March 2011) banned gender as an insurance rating factor across the EU, and the UK retained this post-Brexit. The principle was established: protected characteristics cannot be used in pricing, regardless of actuarial justification. The same logic now applies to ethnicity pricing gaps under the Equality Act 2010 Section 19 (indirect discrimination) and the FCA's evolving EP25/2 framework.

The standard compliance response — remove the characteristic from the model, check model accuracy does not change, write it up — does not demonstrate that your model is *discrimination-free* in the Lindholm et al. (2022) sense. Removing postcode from a motor GLM does not prove that postcode was not acting as an ethnicity proxy. It proves that postcode is not in the model. These are different things.

The correct framework, established by Lindholm, Richman, Tsanakas & Wüthrich (ASTIN Bulletin 52:1, 2022), defines a discrimination-free premium as:

$$h^*(X) = \sum_k f_k(X) \cdot P^*(D=k)$$

where $f_k(X)$ is the group-specific model for group $k$, and $P^*(D=k)$ is the marginal group probability. The price is a weighted average over group-conditional models, with weights equal to the unconditional group distribution rather than the conditional distribution given covariates. This removes the premium variation attributable to the characteristic.

Computing this requires knowing $P(D=k)$ — the group distribution. Which requires the characteristic data you are not allowed to hold.

## What local differential privacy does

LDP is not anonymisation. Anonymisation means destroying the information. LDP means adding *calibrated noise* to the information before it leaves the individual, such that the receiver cannot reliably infer the true value from any single observation, but can recover accurate *aggregate* statistics from many observations.

The mechanism insurance-fairness-ldp uses is k-ary Randomised Response. For a sensitive attribute with $k$ possible values (e.g. 5 broad ethnicity groups), each policyholder (or a trusted third party acting on their behalf) applies the following rule before transmitting their group:

- With probability $\pi = \frac{e^\varepsilon}{k - 1 + e^\varepsilon}$: send the true value
- With probability $1 - \pi$: send a uniformly random value from the other $k-1$ groups

The privacy parameter $\varepsilon$ controls the trade-off. At $\varepsilon = 2$, $\pi \approx 0.88$ for $k=5$: most responses are true. At $\varepsilon = 0.5$, $\pi \approx 0.55$: responses are noisier. The insurer receives $S_{\text{priv}}$ — they never see the true $S$.

From the insurer's side, the received attribute is governed by the transition matrix $T$ where $T_{ij} = P(S_{\text{priv}} = j \mid S = i)$: diagonal entries are $\pi$, off-diagonal entries are $(1-\pi)/(k-1)$. The trick Zhang et al. prove is that minimising the *corrected* group-specific losses — using $T^{-1}$ to account for the noise — is equivalent to minimising the losses on the true (unobserved) data. You can recover the group statistics and train discrimination-free models without ever holding the true attribute.

---

## The accuracy cost

LDP is not free. The accuracy constant $C_1 = \frac{\pi + k - 2}{k\pi - 1}$ governs how much the generalisation bound degrades relative to having access to the true attribute. Zhang et al. (Theorem 4.3) show the bound grows by a factor of $C_1$.

At practical privacy parameters:

| $\varepsilon$ | $k=2$ | $k=5$ |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 2.0 | 0.86 | 2.27 |
| 1.0 | 1.58 | 4.26 |
| 0.5 | 2.57 | 8.19 |

For binary attributes (gender proxy, say) at $\varepsilon = 2$, the accuracy cost is minimal — $C_1 < 1$ means the LDP estimator is *tighter* than the direct estimator in some configurations. For $k=5$ ethnicity groups at $\varepsilon = 0.5$, $C_1 \approx 8$: the estimates will be noisy. The library warns if $C_1 > 5$.

For most UK motor applications, the target is demonstrating *absence of discrimination* rather than maximising model accuracy from the fairness correction. You need the estimates to be accurate enough to show the gap is small — not to price each group to the penny. For this purpose, $\varepsilon = 1$ to $2$ with $k \leq 5$ is workable. The library makes this trade-off visible.

## Usage

The library implements the full pipeline: privatisation at the policyholder side, correction matrix construction, group model training with signed-weight reweighting, and discrimination-free price computation.

```python
from insurance_fairness_ldp import (
    KaryRandomisedResponse,
    CorrectionMatrix,
    LDPDiscriminationFreePrice,
    NoiseRateEstimator,
    LDPFairnessReport,
)
```

**Step 1: Privatise (policyholder/TTP side)**

```python
mechanism = KaryRandomisedResponse(epsilon=1.5, k=5)
# Policyholder applies this before sending to insurer
s_privatised = mechanism.privatise(s_true)  # shape (n,), integer group labels
```

**Step 2: Correct for noise and compute discrimination-free prices (insurer side)**

The insurer receives `s_privatised` and the non-sensitive covariates `X`.

```python
correction = CorrectionMatrix(epsilon=1.5, k=5)
# T_inv: the k×k matrix that debiases group probability estimates
# Off-diagonal entries of T_inv are negative — that's correct and expected
T_inv = correction.T_inv()

pricer = LDPDiscriminationFreePrice(
    base_estimator='ridge',   # or any sklearn-compatible regressor
    epsilon=1.5,
    k=5,
)
pricer.fit(X_train, y_train, s_privatised_train, exposure=exposure_train)

# Discrimination-free premiums: h*(X) = sum_k f_k(X) * P*(D=k)
df_premiums = pricer.predict(X_test)
```

Under the hood, `LDPDiscriminationFreePrice` trains $k$ group-specific models using $\Pi^{-1}$-reweighted samples — the key step that makes the LDP-corrected estimator equivalent to training on true labels. Negative weights are handled via a custom signed-weight WLS solver (sklearn Ridge rejects them; the normal equations remain valid for $\lambda > 0$).

**Step 3: Estimate epsilon if unknown**

If the insurer does not know the privacy parameter (e.g. the TTP applied it without disclosure), the library estimates it from anchor points — observations where the true group membership is effectively certain:

```python
estimator = NoiseRateEstimator(k=5)
estimator.fit(X_train, s_privatised_train, anchor_feature='postcode_density')
epsilon_hat = estimator.epsilon_hat_
```

## The correction matrix arithmetic

The $T^{-1}$ formula is where implementation errors concentrate. Given $\pi = e^\varepsilon / (k - 1 + e^\varepsilon)$:

$$T^{-1}_{ii} = \frac{\pi + k - 2}{k\pi - 1}, \quad T^{-1}_{ij} = \frac{\pi - 1}{k\pi - 1} \text{ for } i \neq j$$

The off-diagonal entries are *negative* because $\pi < 1$. The common mistake is negating them to make them positive — which produces a matrix that looks reasonable but fails the $T \cdot T^{-1} = I$ test. The library ships with an explicit test for this. Row sums of $T^{-1}$ equal 1 (signed measure property), which is another useful check.

```python
import numpy as np

correction = CorrectionMatrix(epsilon=1.5, k=5)
T = correction.T()
T_inv = correction.T_inv()

# Both of these should hold:
assert np.allclose(T @ T_inv, np.eye(5))
assert np.allclose(T_inv.sum(axis=1), np.ones(5))
```

## The fairness report

`LDPFairnessReport` produces a structured HTML and JSON output formatted around the FCA EP25/2 and GDPR compliance questions:

```python
report = LDPFairnessReport(
    epsilon=1.5,
    k=5,
    group_labels=['White British', 'Asian', 'Black', 'Mixed', 'Other'],
)
report.fit(pricer, X_test, y_test, s_privatised_test)
report.generate(output_path='./ldp_fairness_report.html')
```

The report covers: accuracy constant $C_1$ and its interpretation; group probability estimates before and after correction; calibration by estimated group; the LDP privacy guarantee and what it means for GDPR Article 9; and the Equality Act s.19 proportionate justification assessment template. The regulatory context is embedded in the output, not left to the reader to supply.

## The multi-party architecture requirement

The formal GDPR Article 9 guarantee is only airtight in the multi-party architecture. A Trusted Third Party holds the true sensitive attributes and applies the LDP mechanism before transmission. The insurer receives only the privatised values. Because no single observation reliably reveals the true value, the insurer cannot reconstruct the original attribute even in principle.

If the insurer controls the privatisation step themselves — which is the single-party mode the library also supports — the formal guarantee weakens. The insurer could in principle store the pre-privatisation value. The library documents this prominently:

```python
# Single-party mode: insurer applies privatisation
# WARNING: formal Article 9 protection requires TTP architecture
mechanism = KaryRandomisedResponse(epsilon=1.5, k=5, mode='single_party')
```

For UK motor insurers looking to satisfy the FCA's EP25/2 expectation without a full TTP deployment, the single-party mode is useful for *internal auditing* — running the discrimination-free pricing analysis on your own data without the intent to process it for live pricing. Whether that constitutes processing under Article 9 is a legal question, not a technical one.

## Relationship to the fairness stack

`insurance-fairness-ldp` sits at the GDPR-compliant end of the fairness stack:

- [`insurance-fairness`](https://github.com/burning-cost/insurance-fairness): proxy discrimination detection using observable data. Uses the characteristic if you have it.
- [`insurance-fairness-diag`](https://github.com/burning-cost/insurance-fairness-diag): D_proxy scalar and Shapley attribution. Quantifies how much discrimination exists and which rating factors carry it.
- [`insurance-fairness-ot`](https://github.com/burning-cost/insurance-fairness-ot): Lindholm marginalisation and Wasserstein barycenter correction. Requires observable group distribution estimates.
- **`insurance-fairness-ldp`**: the LDP layer. For when you do not have the attribute and cannot acquire it.

The intended workflow for a UK motor insurer facing FCA scrutiny on ethnicity pricing: use `insurance-fairness-ldp` in TTP mode to establish that the group distribution estimates are unbiased; use `insurance-fairness-ot` (Lindholm marginalisation) with those estimates to produce discrimination-free prices; use `insurance-fairness-diag` to document the D_proxy reduction; produce the `LDPFairnessReport` as the regulatory artefact.
