The UK motor market completed eight consecutive quarters of premium reductions ending November 2025. Average premiums fell from £995 at peak (December 2023) to £726 by that point — a 27% decline, or £269 off the average policy in less than two years.

EY's December 2025 motor results analysis forecasts a net combined ratio of 101% for 2025, rising to 111% for 2026. For context: 2024 delivered an NCR of 97%, the first underwriting profit since 2021. That margin lasted exactly one year before the competitive cycle erased it.

This is not a surprise. It is the predictable end-state of a market that found a genuine tailwind — whiplash reform — priced it in aggressively, over-corrected, and is now running into the structural cost pressures it had been discounting.

---

## The NCR history, briefly

The 2022–2023 cycle was severe. NCR hit 109.5% in 2022 and deteriorated to 112.7% in 2023, the worst result on record for UK motor. Claims inflation compounded at a time when earned premiums were still reflecting earlier softer pricing. The recovery was sharp: a premium hardening of +14% in 2024 drove the NCR back to 97%, the fastest turnaround the market had seen.

| Year | NCR (%) | Comment |
|------|---------|---------|
| 2020 | 90.3 | Best result on record |
| 2021 | 96.6 | Whiplash reform begins |
| 2022 | 109.5 | Inflation surge; worst since at least 2010 |
| 2023 | 112.7 | Worst year on record |
| 2024 | 97.0 | Premium hardening bites; profitable |
| 2025 | 101.0 | Forecast (EY, Dec 2025) |
| 2026 | 111.0 | Forecast (EY, Dec 2025) |

The 2026 forecast of 111% puts the market essentially back where it was in 2023, with no obvious repair mechanism in sight given the current competitive dynamic.

## What the premium cycle actually looked like

WTW's Confused.com/WTW Car Insurance Price Index (data to November 2025) gives the cleanest picture of where premiums went quarter by quarter. The headline: eight consecutive quarterly declines from a peak of £995 in December 2023. The deceleration is notable — the September–November 2025 quarter saw just a 1% fall (£9), the smallest move in the entire sequence.

Consumer Intelligence's independent tracker, which compiles real Rank 1–5 quotes from PCWs and is used as the reference series by both the ONS and the FCA, shows similar shape. At October 2025, the average top-5 quoted premium was £1,210, down 9.1% year-on-year. The quarterly figure had nearly flatlined at -0.5%, with under-25s already showing a +3.6% quarterly uptick — the first cohort to price back up.

WTW's Tim Rourke described the September–November 2025 data as indicating "a continued deceleration in the pace of price reductions." That is market-analyst language for: the floor is here.

EY's estimate is that the 2025 premium reduction averaged 10% year-on-year, equivalent to about £54 per policy. For 2026, it forecasts a 3% increase (£15 per policy average) — which, assuming the 111% NCR forecast is correct, means premium recovery is running at roughly one-quarter the rate needed to close the gap.

## Eight quarters of cuts. Two of them were justified.

We want to be precise about this.

The 2022–2023 premium hardening was a necessary correction to a book that had been systematically underpriced relative to claims costs. Claims inflation in that period was running at 10–14% year-on-year. The hardening was late, compressed, and ugly, but directionally correct.

The first four or five quarters of the subsequent softening cycle — roughly Q1 2024 through Q2 2025 — reflected a genuine improvement in claims experience. Whiplash reform had materially reduced bodily injury (BI) spend. The 15% tariff uplift applied to whiplash claims from 31 May 2025 (Whiplash Injury Amendment Regulations 2025) is a partial reversal, but the reforms had by then already delivered several years of structural savings.

The WTW data is explicit: BI claims as a share of total insurer spend fell from 16% in 2021 to 9% by late 2025. At a UK motor market writing roughly £12–13bn in annual GWP, that is a structural shift worth in the region of £800m–£1bn in annual claims savings. The premium reductions that absorbed this tailwind were not irrational.

The problem is that the market continued cutting past the point where the claims improvement justified it. The last three to four quarters of premium reductions — from roughly Q2 2025 onwards — do not correspond to any improvement in the underlying cost base. They correspond to competitive pressure, particularly on price comparison websites, where margin compression has been most acute. The under-25 cohort re-inflating at +3.6% quarterly is the market's own leading indicator that something has to give.

## The claims cost picture

The ABI's Q2 2025 data shows total claims payouts of £3.1bn in the quarter, up from £3.0bn in Q1 2025 and matching what the ABI described as a "record-breaking" quarterly payout. Repair costs accounted for £2.1bn of that Q2 figure — 68% of total claims.

The structural pressures are well-documented at this point, and they are not going away:

**Vehicle complexity and EV.** The average repair bill for an accident-damaged vehicle has risen over 35% in three years. EVs cost around 25% more to repair than equivalent ICE vehicles and take 14% longer. Specialist battery diagnostics and repair require skilled technicians the industry does not have enough of. Even ADAS-equipped ICE vehicles — now a large share of the parc — require recalibration of radar and camera systems after any bodywork repair. That is additional labour time on every claim.

**Materials inflation.** Specialist paints, lacquers, and fillers used in bodywork repairs rose by over 30% in two years through 2024. Those costs do not reverse when CPI comes down.

**Theft.** The average theft claim reached £11,800 in Q2 2025 (+3% on Q1), with total theft-related payouts at £142m in Q3. Keyless entry relay attacks remain the dominant vector, particularly on newer vehicles with high-value electronics.

**Courtesy car duration.** Average courtesy car hire periods have extended by over 30% in two years, driven by repair shop backlogs and longer EV repair windows. This is a significant secondary cost that does not feature prominently in headline claims figures but contributes materially to incurred losses.

The one category that has been holding: bodily injury. BI claims rose just 7% in value from 2019 to 2023, while every other category rose over 40%. The whiplash reforms were responsible for that structural suppression. The 15% tariff uplift from May 2025, combined with the Court of Appeal judgment on mixed-injury claims (which brought settlement values close to pre-reform levels for claims involving additional minor physical injuries), suggests the BI tailwind is now exhausted and beginning to reverse.

---

## The whiplash tailwind is gone

The 2021 whiplash reforms were the most significant structural pricing change to UK motor in at least a decade. The Civil Liability Act 2018, implemented via the Whiplash Injury Regulations 2021, capped soft-tissue injury compensation at fixed tariff amounts, removed the right to legal representation for straightforward claims, and routed claims through the Official Injury Claim portal.

HM Treasury's statutory report (March 2025) assessed that the reforms saved motorists £15–31 per policy between 2021 and 2024. Insurers confirmed that the Act led to premiums being approximately £31 lower than they would otherwise have been.

That saving is now being clawed back. Three developments are working against it simultaneously:

1. **The 15% tariff increase** from 31 May 2025, applying to all new claims. This reflects the higher-than-expected inflation between 2021 and 2024 and includes a forward buffer until the 2027 review.

2. **The mixed injury ruling.** The Court of Appeal judgment on claims with both whiplash and minor additional physical injuries has taken values back close to pre-reform levels for this class. Claimant lawyers have adapted quickly to structure pleadings accordingly.

3. **The Government's post-implementation review** (call for evidence, October 2025) suggests further reform is live. Any rollback — particularly on the tariff structure or representation rights — would immediately re-inflate BI costs.

The consensus view at the time, in 2021, was that the market would fully price in the whiplash saving within 18–24 months. That is roughly what happened. The mistake was treating it as a permanent structural improvement rather than a time-limited tailwind. Pricing teams that built the reform savings into their rated risk costs permanently, without flagging the review risk, are now sitting on a cost assumption that is actively deteriorating.

## What this means for pricing teams

The aggregate picture is clear enough. The harder question is: where are you relative to the market, and how exposed is your specific book to the cost trends above?

**Rate adequacy by cohort.** The EY and WTW data are market averages. The distribution around that average is what matters for your book. If your mix is weighted towards ADAS-equipped vehicles, EVs, or urban under-30s (who have seen larger premium reductions: -16% YoY for under-25s at November 2025), your claims cost inflation is likely above the market average. If you are weighted towards older rural drivers, your claims profile is different, but your premium reductions may have been comparable.

**Whiplash reform exposure.** How much of your BI cost reduction between 2021 and 2025 did you pass through as a rate reduction, versus hold as margin? If you can segment this at a policy level — BI frequency by region, claim type, legal representation flag — you can quantify how exposed you are to the May 2025 tariff uplift and the mixed injury ruling.

**Earned versus written.** The EY forecast for 2025's NCR reflects premiums written in 2024 and early 2025, when the rate reductions were accelerating. The worst of the earned premium drag will show up in 2025 and 2026 accounts, as the softer policies earn through. If your book is renewing at a 12-month cadence, you are carrying this on most of your portfolio through most of 2025.

**Theft topology.** If your portfolio is overweight London, the West Midlands, and the North-West — the three highest-premium regions per Consumer Intelligence's October 2025 data (North-West at £1,680 average, London at £1,439) — you are disproportionately exposed to relay theft. These regions combine high vehicle density with high theft frequency. The ABI theft cost data (£142m in Q3, £11.8k average claim) is not spread uniformly across the country.

## Benchmarking your book against the market

The [`insurance-monitoring`](https://github.com/burning-cost/insurance-monitoring) library provides the monitoring infrastructure for tracking where your book sits relative to these market trends. The `portfolio_drift` module compares your current written mix against a reference period across any feature set — vehicle type, region, NCB band, driver age — and surfaces the segments where your composition has shifted. That matters here because the market average claims inflation figure obscures significant segment-level variation.

For rate adequacy assessment at segment level, the complement is [`insurance-credibility`](https://github.com/burning-cost/insurance-credibility). The challenge in a market moving this fast is that segment-level claims data is sparse: you have enough credibility to observe the overall book deteriorating, but not necessarily enough to distinguish whether the AD cost inflation is worse in your EV segment than your ICE segment, or whether your BI recovery is correlated with the geographic distribution of mixed-injury claims. `insurance-credibility` implements Bühlmann-Straub and its extensions, which is what you want when you need to blend sparse segment observations with a market prior.

The workflow we would recommend:

1. Identify segments where your written volume has changed more than 5% as a share of portfolio since Q1 2024 (when the premium softening began). These are the segments where competitive pressure most likely distorted your pricing.
2. For each of those segments, compare your incurred loss ratio trend against the period before the softening. Any segment running above trend by more than one standard deviation of historical volatility is a candidate for a rate action.
3. Flag your BI cost explicitly. Run the 2021–2025 reform saving as a separate line item in your claims cost analysis. If that line is now moving adverse — tariff uplift showing in new notifications, mixed injury claims increasing as a share of BI — you know the whiplash tailwind has reversed and you can quantify it.
4. On theft: pull your relay-theft claims separately. The average claim of £11.8k is a significant event. If your policy mix has shifted towards higher-value vehicles in high-theft postcodes, your theft load needs a review.

## The view from here

The 2022–2023 cycle happened fast. The market underpriced, then over-corrected, then softened again before the correction had fully earned through. The 2026 forecast of 111% NCR suggests the market has done something similar: found profitability in 2024, immediately softened to the point of structural loss-making, and is now watching claims cost inflation compound against an inadequate rate base.

The difference from 2022 is that there is no large identifiable shock to blame. In 2022, the BI surge following the pandemic was visible in claims notifications well before it showed in earned premiums. This time, the deterioration is the accumulation of several structural trends — vehicle complexity, materials costs, EV repair, the end of the whiplash tailwind — none of which is individually catastrophic but which together are moving faster than the competitive market has been willing to price.

EY expects the 3% average premium increase in 2026 to fall well short of what is needed. We agree. A 111% NCR implies roughly 11 percentage points of rate inadequacy relative to break-even, and the earned premium from the soft 2025 period will be dragging on results well into 2027.

The actuarially justified response is immediate: rates up, rate change skewed to high-complexity vehicles and high-theft regions, BI load reviewed and forward-loaded for the tariff reversal. Whether that is what the market delivers is a different question.
